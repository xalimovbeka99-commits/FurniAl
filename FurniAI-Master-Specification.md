# FurniAI — Master Product & Engineering Specification

*UAE custom-furniture platform: gallery → customize → pay → automatic factory production.*

---

## 1. What FurniAI is

FurniAI is a web platform where a customer picks a piece of furniture from a large visual gallery, customizes it (size, material, doors, drawers, finish), sees a live price, pays, and the system automatically generates a complete factory production pack (cut list + drilling spec + production PDF) and dispatches it for manufacturing — with no human re-drawing the design in between.

The product sits on one core asset: a **3D furniture configurator** where every model is *parametric and manufacturable* — meaning anything a customer can see and customize can be cut, priced, and built, with no separate "design-to-production" translation step.

The business is **custom furniture that customers configure and the factory then builds**. That single fact drives every architectural decision below.

---

## 2. The core principle (read this first)

Everything in FurniAI flows through **one data object: the `FurnitureConfig`.**

```
  Gallery / AI                                  ┌─→ 3D model      (what the customer sees)
   selection   ─→  FurnitureConfig  ─→  Store ──┼─→ Live price    (what they pay)
                   (single source                ├─→ Cut list      (what the factory cuts)
                    of truth)                     └─→ Production PDF (how it's built)
```

Change one parameter → the store updates → the 3D model, the price, the cut list, and the production documents all follow automatically. Nothing is hardcoded; nothing can be shown that can't be built.

### The three honest engineering truths this design is built on

These were tested against the research and they are non-negotiable:

**Truth 1 — "100% accurate" comes from determinism, not from AI.**
A reliable manufacturing system means *the same input always produces the same correct, buildable output.* That is a property of **deterministic code**, not of an AI model (which is probabilistic and can confidently produce wrong geometry). Therefore the geometry, the price, and the CNC/cut data are generated by pure code. AI never touches them.

**Truth 2 — A photo cannot become a dimensionally-exact 3D model.**
A single image contains no real-world measurements (this is a geometric impossibility, confirmed across the computer-vision literature — even the best models are ~24–35% off on furniture dimensions). So the system matches a picture's **type, layout, proportions, style, and colour family**, then starts from **standard manufacturable sizes**, which the customer confirms. "Same look, made to measure" is real. "Same exact sizes as the photo" is not.

**Truth 3 — Parametric beats agent-drives-CAD for a configurable product.**
An AI agent *can* script 3ds Max / AutoCAD / SketchUp / Blender headless in the cloud — that part is technically real. But it outputs a **frozen mesh** that isn't editable, isn't tied to a cut list, and isn't guaranteed manufacturable. For a product customers customize and a factory builds, the **parametric generator** (build geometry from validated parameters in code) is the correct core. CAD-style mesh generation is reserved for optional marketing renders, never the production path.

> **Where AI genuinely belongs:** turning a fuzzy human request or a gallery image into *structured parameters*, and *recommending* templates. Two narrow jobs it's good at. Everything load-bearing is deterministic code.

## 3. The customer journey (end to end)

| Step | What the customer does | What the system does | AI involved? |
|------|------------------------|----------------------|--------------|
| 1. Browse | Opens the gallery of 100–500 designs | Shows curated, filterable templates | AI *recommends/sorts* (optional) |
| 2. Select | Clicks a design ("modern walnut wardrobe") | Loads that template's `FurnitureConfig` into the builder | AI *interprets* image/label → params (optional refine) |
| 3. Customize | Adjusts size, material, doors, drawers, finish on left/right panels | 3D model + live price update on every change | No — deterministic |
| 4. Finish | Presses "Finish & get price" | Locks the configuration; shows final price | No — deterministic |
| 5. Pay | Pays online | **Freezes the exact config to the order** (immutable) | No |
| 6. Produce | — (nothing) | Auto-generates cut list CSV + production PDF, dispatches to factory | No — deterministic |
| 7. Build | — | Factory operator opens the production pack and manufactures | No |

The only humans in the loop are the customer (steps 1–5) and the factory operator (step 7). Steps 6 is fully automated.

### 4.2 Data flow on a purchase

```
select template ─→ loadConfig() ─→ [customer edits] ─→ Finish
   ─→ freeze config + OrderID ─→ payment ─→ on success:
        ├─ buildGeometry()  → partsToCutList()  → cutlist.csv
        └─ production data  → production PDF (English, mm)
   ─→ store files + email/dispatch to factory
```

Every transform after "freeze" is a pure function of the frozen config. Re-running them on the same order always yields identical files.

## 5. The 3D model system

### 5.1 Parametric, not hardcoded

There are **no hardcoded kitchen/wardrobe meshes.** Every model is generated at runtime from its `FurnitureConfig` by a pure function (`buildGeometry`) that returns a list of panels — each with a role, size in metres, and position. The same panel list is:
- rendered as 3D boxes in the viewport (react-three-fiber), and
- converted to a millimetre cut list for the factory.

This is what keeps the model *manufacturable by construction*: it can only ever be made of real, cuttable panels.

**No CAD software is used in the product.** Not the old site (hardcoded meshes), not the new one (parametric code). 3ds Max / AutoCAD / SketchUp / Blender were evaluated and deliberately rejected for the core, because they output frozen, non-editable, non-manufacturable meshes. The only place a CAD-style mesh could ever appear is an optional, clearly-labelled marketing render — never in the customize, price, or production path.

### 5.2 How a model is built from parameters

A piece is a **carcass** (sides, top, bottom, back, optional plinth) divided into **modules** — vertical sections left to right. Each module is one of:
- **door** (1–4 doors, hinge left/right, shelves inside)
- **drawerBank** (1–6 drawer rows)
- **openShelf** (0–8 shelves)
- **applianceGap** (e.g. space for an oven in a kitchen run)

Module widths are stored as **proportions** (share of total width), not fixed sizes — so when the customer changes the overall width, the layout re-flows correctly and stays buildable. Dividers, shelves, doors, and drawer fronts are placed by maths derived from the parameters.

### 5.3 The gallery of 100–500 models

The gallery is **not** 500 hand-built files, and **not** 500 live AI generations. It is:

1. **8–12 type families** authored once (wardrobe, kitchen run, cabinet, vanity, shelving, TV unit, office desk, bed, etc.), each a parametric template.
2. **Hundreds of variants** produced by changing parameters on those families (width, door count, material, style, handle). 12 families × ~40 parameter presets = ~480 gallery items — all data, all instantly loadable, all manufacturable.

AI's role in the gallery is **selection and recommendation** (match a customer's intent or uploaded inspiration to the closest templates), which it does reliably. Authoring brand-new families "from zero" with AI assistance is an **internal design tool for the FurniAI team** — new families are reviewed and validated before entering the gallery, never generated live for a paying customer.

### 5.5 Why the new system changes when the old one wouldn't

The old site's models were **hardcoded**, so every new wardrobe or kitchen meant editing code — slow, fragile, and the reason changes "wouldn't take." In the new system, **a design is data, not code.** Every gallery item is a `FurnitureConfig` preset in one file (`designs.js`). To add a new wardrobe, kitchen, or anything else, you add an entry there — and the 3D model, price, cut list, and factory PDF all follow automatically. The code (geometry, store, panels, pricing, production) never changes. *Trying new things = adding/editing presets, repeatedly, until it's right* — exactly the iterate-from-scratch workflow intended, with the system staying stable underneath.

### 5.4 The builder UI (customization page)

Three regions: **left panel │ 3D viewport │ right panel.**

- **Left — Structure:** furniture type, and per-section (module) door/drawer/shelf counts and hinge side. Clicking a door or drawer in the 3D view selects its section.
- **3D viewport:** orbit/zoom; the live parametric model; click-to-select.
- **Right — Appearance & size:** width/height/depth sliders (snapped to manufacturable ranges), material, handle, door type, LED, plinth, and a live indicative price.

## 6. Where AI is used — and where it is deliberately not

| Job | AI used? | Why |
|-----|----------|-----|
| Recommend gallery templates to a customer | ✅ Yes | Pattern-matching intent → catalog; AI is good at this |
| Turn a text request ("modern 2.4m walnut wardrobe") into parameters | ✅ Yes | Language → structured data; AI is good at this |
| Refine a config to match a chosen gallery *image* (vision) | ✅ Optional | Type/layout/colour inference; useful, but only *over* a validated template |
| Conversational sales / WhatsApp quoting | ✅ Yes | Calls the deterministic pricing engine as a tool; never invents prices |
| **Generate the 3D geometry** | ❌ No | Must be deterministic & manufacturable |
| **Calculate the price the customer pays** | ❌ No | Must be exact and reproducible |
| **Produce the cut list / drilling / CNC data** | ❌ No | A drill goes where the maths says, not where a model guesses |

**Safety rule for every AI output:** it is merged *over* a known-good template, then validated against the catalog (every material/handle/door type checked, every dimension clamped to a manufacturable range, every count coerced to a sane integer, module ratios renormalised). Anything the AI gets wrong degrades to a safe default instead of producing an unbuildable model.

## 7. The data model — `FurnitureConfig`

The one object that flows through the whole system. Dimensions in metres (Three.js world units); converted to mm for production.

```
FurnitureConfig = {
  type:          "wardrobe" | "kitchen" | "cabinet" | "shelves" |
                 "office" | "bed" | "table" | "dressing_table",
  style:         "modern" | "classic" | "handleless" | ...,
  material:      catalog key (oak, walnut, white MDF, ...),
  handleStyle:   "gold_bar" | "silver_knob" | "black_strip" | "hidden_push" | "chrome",
  doorType:      "solid_panel" | "glass_panel" | "full_mirror" | "frosted_glass",
  ledLighting:   "off" | "warm" | "cool" | "rgb",
  hasPlinth:     boolean,
  plinthHeight:  number (m),
  dimensions:    { width, height, depth },          // metres, clamped to ranges
  modules: [                                         // left → right
    { kind: "door" | "drawerBank" | "openShelf" | "applianceGap",
      widthRatio:  number,        // share of total width (normalised to sum 1)
      doorCount:   0–4,
      drawerRows:  0–6,
      shelfCount:  0–8,
      hingeSide:   "left" | "right",
      slideType:   "hinged" | "sliding" }
  ],
  ai: { source: "default"|"template"|"text"|"vision",  // provenance
        confidence: number|null,
        assumptions: string[] }
}
```

Storing module layout as **ratios + counts** (not absolute mm) is the key decision: the same configuration survives a width change and re-flows against manufacturing rules.

## 8. Pricing model

Deterministic, transparent, in AED. The customer-facing live price and the authoritative server quote use the same model.

```
material   = panel_area_m² × material_cost_per_m²
hardware   = handles + hinges + drawer slides + hanger rod
             + fixings(per m²) + LED + door surcharges
labour     = labour_base(type) × complexity(type)
subtotal   = material + hardware + labour
margin     = subtotal × 0.35
delivery   = zone rate (Dubai 50, Abu Dhabi 70, Sharjah 60, ...)
TOTAL      = subtotal + margin + delivery   (AED)
```

Material and hardware costs, furniture-type defaults, and delivery zones live in one editable catalog file (`knowledgeBase.js`) so a non-developer can update pricing. The price a customer pays is **frozen at payment** and never recomputed.

## 9. Factory / production pipeline

### 9.1 On payment: freeze + generate

When payment succeeds, the system:
1. **Freezes** the exact `FurnitureConfig` to the order with a unique **OrderID** (what they paid for is now immutable).
2. Generates the **cut list CSV** and the **production PDF** as pure functions of the frozen config.
3. Stores both and dispatches them to the factory (email or portal).

### 9.2 Cut list CSV (for the operator / BAZIS entry)

English, millimetres. One row per panel:

```
OrderID, PartID, PartName, Material, Length(mm), Width(mm),
Thickness(mm), Qty, EdgeBanding, Grain, Module
```

CSV because spreadsheets, panel saws, and BAZIS all ingest it cleanly. (If a specific optimizer/saw later needs exact column names, the format remaps in one place.)

### 9.3 Production PDF (the human-readable factory pack)

English, mm, OrderID on every page for traceability. Contents:
- Project header (OrderID, furniture type, overall dimensions, material, date)
- Cut list table
- Drilling & hardware spec (hinge cups, shelf pins, confirmat positions in mm)
- Elevations with dimensions
- Parts summary

### 9.4 Factory handoff: how it reaches the machines

The factory uses **BAZIS-Mebelshik** for nesting and CNC. Two facts shaped the design:
- BAZIS prefers to *do* nesting/CNC itself rather than receive finished CNC files, and its 3D-DXF import is restricted (SketchUp-flavoured DXF, via a Woodsoft add-on).
- The chosen, most robust handoff is therefore **PDF + CSV**, which the operator works from / enters into BAZIS. This avoids dependence on BAZIS's finicky importer and survives a future change of CNC tooling.

**Traceability rule:** OrderID appears in the CSV filename and on every PDF page, so any cut on the floor traces back to the exact config that produced it.

1. **Finish the spine → gallery** — variant generator + gallery page; prove "pick → customize → price" with no AI. De-risks everything.

Each stage ships a working product; none depends on a later stage.

## 13. Honest limitations & risks

- **Single-image dimensions are inferred, not measured.** The platform sets sizes from manufacturable defaults and asks the customer to confirm. For fitting an existing alcove, a real measurement step (or LiDAR scan) is required — a photo can't supply it.
- **AI colour from photos is unreliable** (lighting/white balance). Treat AI colour as a *family* suggestion confirmed against a real swatch — UAE buyers judge on the physical finish.
- **Pricing must stay calibrated.** The catalog uses placeholder economics; the biggest accuracy lever is validating each furniture type's panel-area factor against a real cut list. Keep the app catalog and the skill catalog in sync.
- **BAZIS import is constrained.** That's why the handoff is PDF + CSV rather than DXF; revisit only if direct file import is proven on the factory's exact BAZIS setup.
- **The gallery is templates + variants, not infinite AI creativity.** This is a deliberate trade of "magic" for reliability and manufacturability — the right trade for a company that has to actually build what it sells.
